## PART 1: RESEARCH SYNTHESIS — Why RAG Specifically?

Before the Q&A, the critical mental model you must carry into any interview: RAG is the right choice over fine-tuning or standard prompting in three conditions — the knowledge is too large for a context window, it changes frequently enough that retraining is impractical, or access control means different users must see different subsets of it. Every enterprise use case below satisfies at least two of these three.

* * *

### The 12 High-Value Non-Obvious Enterprise RAG Scenarios

**1\. Sales — Deal intelligence and competitive positioning**

What's retrieved: Historical win/loss reports, competitor battle cards, pricing exception records, objection-handling transcripts from Gong calls, and customer case studies. Why RAG wins here: competitive intelligence is updated weekly as products change. Fine-tuning a model on this would require retraining constantly. Standard prompting can't fit 500 battle cards into a context window. RAG retrieves the three most relevant battle cards for a specific competitor, in the specific deal context.

**2\. Finance — Loan covenant monitoring and regulatory filing interpretation**

What's retrieved: The specific debt agreement clauses, definitions of financial ratios, current period actuals from the data warehouse, and historical compliance records. The non-obvious insight: this isn't a document search problem. It's a reasoning-over-retrieved-numbers problem. RAG retrieves both the covenant definition text and the current financial metrics, then the LLM reasons about whether the covenant is met. Neither fine-tuning nor a pure LLM could do this — the model has no knowledge of this company's specific covenant terms.

**3\. Legal — M &A due diligence and contract clause extraction**

What's retrieved: Specific clause language across a corpus of thousands of contracts, legal precedents from past negotiations, regulatory guidance documents. The scale problem is the key: a typical M&A due diligence review involves 10,000+ documents. No fine-tuned model knows these documents. No context window fits them. RAG enables queries like "find all contracts with unlimited liability clauses where the counterparty is in a sanctioned jurisdiction" — extracting structured signal from unstructured legal text at scale.

**4\. HR — Policy Q &A and employee relations case retrieval**

What's retrieved: HR policy documents, past ER case resolutions with outcomes, compensation band data, and local labour law references by jurisdiction. The non-obvious value: HR advice must be consistent across HRBPs. A RAG system grounded in official policy and past precedent enforces consistency in a way that an HRBP relying on memory cannot. It also surfaces relevant past cases that a busy HRBP might not recall.

**5\. Product — Customer feedback synthesis grounded in evidence**

What's retrieved: NPS verbatim comments, Zendesk ticket clusters, Gong call transcripts where specific features were mentioned, UserVoice requests, and beta feedback reports. The problem it solves: PMs making roadmap decisions based on the loudest voice in the room rather than the strongest signal across thousands of data points. RAG enables "show me everything customers have said about the export feature in the last 90 days, ranked by sentiment and frequency."

**6\. Engineering — Codebase Q &A and incident post-mortem retrieval**

What's retrieved: Architecture Decision Records, past incident post-mortems with root cause and resolution, internal runbooks, and code comments/documentation. The non-obvious scenario: on-call engineers at 2am don't need a code generator — they need to know whether this specific error pattern has been seen before and what fixed it last time. RAG over the incident history is a radically different and more valuable application than code generation.

**7\. Customer Success — Account intelligence before renewal calls**

What's retrieved: Full account health history, support ticket history and resolution times, product usage telemetry, past QBR notes, executive sponsor change history, and renewal risk scores over time. The problem: a CSM inheriting an account or preparing for a renewal call currently has to manually trawl through CRM notes, Gainsight, and email. RAG assembles a grounded account narrative in seconds, surfacing the relevant signals a human would miss.

**8\. Compliance — Regulatory change mapping and audit evidence retrieval**

What's retrieved: New regulatory text (e.g. a new GDPR guidance or SEC rule update), existing internal control documentation, past audit findings, and policy documents. The high-value scenario: when a new regulation is published, the compliance team needs to know which internal controls are affected, whether existing controls are sufficient, and what evidence exists of compliance. RAG maps the new regulation to the specific internal control inventory — something that currently requires weeks of manual analysis.

**9\. Supply Chain — Supplier risk and disruption intelligence**

What's retrieved: Supplier financial health data from credit agencies, news feeds mentioning supplier names, supplier contract terms (force majeure clauses, delivery SLAs), and historical disruption records. The non-obvious framing: this is a multi-source retrieval problem where the answer requires synthesising information across financial data, news, and contract language — something no single system currently does for supply chain teams.

**10\. M &A / Strategy — Integration playbook and deal institutional memory**

What's retrieved: Past deal integration post-mortems, cultural assessment frameworks used in past deals, synergy model templates, and documents from the current target's virtual data room. The problem: institutional memory from past deals walks out the door every time a deal team member leaves. RAG over deal archives preserves and makes searchable the lessons from every transaction the company has ever done.

**11\. IT / Security — Vulnerability triage against the actual environment**

What's retrieved: CVE details and severity scores, the company's specific asset inventory (CMDB), patch history for affected systems, and security policy exceptions. The non-obvious value: a CVE is publicly known severity-9.8, but whether it's actually critical for this company depends on whether they run the affected software version and whether that system is internet-exposed. RAG over the internal CMDB and CVE database gives environment-specific risk assessment.

**12\. R &D / IP — Prior art retrieval and patent landscape mapping**

What's retrieved: Patent filings from USPTO/EPO databases, internal research documents, academic papers, and competitive product announcements. The problem it solves: patent searches currently require specialised patent attorneys and weeks of work. RAG can surface relevant prior art across millions of patents in minutes, fundamentally changing the economics of IP due diligence.

## 🟢 FOUNDATIONAL QUESTIONS

* * *

**F1: Why is RAG better than fine-tuning for enterprise use cases involving frequently changing private data?**

Fine-tuning bakes knowledge into model weights. This means every time the data changes — a new policy is published, a contract is amended, a competitor launches a product — you must retrain and redeploy the model. In enterprise environments where knowledge changes weekly or daily, this is operationally impractical and extremely expensive.

RAG separates knowledge from the model. The model's weights stay fixed; the knowledge base is updated independently. Adding a new contract to the vector store takes seconds. More importantly, fine-tuning cannot give the model access to genuinely private, proprietary data — you'd have to trust the model provider with your confidential documents. RAG keeps sensitive data in your own infrastructure and retrieves it at query time.

There is also a traceability argument: a fine-tuned model cannot tell you where its answer came from. A RAG system can cite the exact document chunk that grounded the response — critical for compliance, legal, and audit use cases where attribution is not optional.

The right framing: fine-tuning is for teaching the model how to behave (style, format, domain reasoning patterns). RAG is for giving the model what to know. Most enterprise use cases need the latter, not the former.

* * *

**F2: Why can't you just put all enterprise documents into the LLM context window instead of building a RAG system?**

Three reasons: scale, cost, and latency.

Scale: a typical enterprise has millions of documents — contracts, emails, Confluence pages, Slack archives, code repositories. Even the largest context windows (1-2 million tokens as of 2025) cannot hold this. A single large contract corpus alone would overflow any context window.

Cost: LLM pricing is per token. Stuffing 500 documents into every query context multiplies API costs by orders of magnitude compared to retrieving the relevant 5 documents and including only those. At enterprise query volumes this becomes completely uneconomical.

Latency and quality: counterintuitively, giving a model too much context often degrades answer quality. Models suffer from the "lost in the middle" problem — information buried in the middle of a long context is retrieved less reliably than information at the start or end. RAG retrieves only the relevant chunks, making the relevant signal density much higher.

There is one scenario where full-context approaches make sense: when the document set is small and fixed (e.g. testing a single contract). For anything at enterprise scale, RAG is architecturally necessary.

* * *

**F3: What is the difference between semantic search and keyword search in RAG retrieval, and when does each matter?**

Keyword search (BM25, TF-IDF) matches on exact terms. It is fast, interpretable, and highly precise when the user knows the exact terminology. "Section 14.2 indemnification" will find that clause reliably. It fails on synonyms, paraphrase, and conceptual queries.

Semantic search uses dense vector embeddings to find conceptually similar content regardless of exact wording. "What happens if we can't deliver on time?" will retrieve force majeure clauses even if those words don't appear in the query. It handles natural language queries well but can retrieve irrelevant content that is semantically adjacent to the query without being relevant.

In enterprise RAG, neither alone is sufficient. The winning architecture is hybrid retrieval — run both in parallel, then use a reranker model to score and merge the combined candidate set. This gives you the precision of keyword matching for known-term queries and the recall of semantic search for conceptual queries.

When each matters by function: legal and compliance teams often know exact clause terminology → keyword search is critical. Customer success or HR users asking natural language questions → semantic search is primary. Compliance regulatory mapping needs both, because regulations use specific defined terms that must be matched exactly, but the mapping to internal controls is conceptual.

* * *

**F4: What is chunking strategy in RAG and why does it matter more than most teams realise?**

Chunking is the process of splitting source documents into the pieces that get embedded and stored in the vector database. It is one of the highest-leverage decisions in RAG system design and one of the most commonly under-thought.

The fundamental tension: small chunks give high retrieval precision (you retrieve exactly the relevant sentence) but low retrieval context (the LLM doesn't have enough surrounding text to understand it). Large chunks give more context but reduce retrieval precision and blow up the context window.

Common strategies and when to use them:

Fixed-size chunking (e.g. 512 tokens with 50-token overlap) is the default. It works adequately for homogeneous documents like FAQ articles. It fails badly on structured documents like contracts where a clause might span exactly 200 tokens or 800 tokens depending on its complexity — fixed splitting cuts clauses mid-sentence.

Semantic chunking splits at natural boundaries — paragraph breaks, section headers, sentence endings — rather than at a fixed token count. Better for legal, policy, and technical documents.

Hierarchical chunking maintains parent-child relationships. A contract section is the parent chunk; individual clauses are child chunks. Retrieval finds the relevant clause; the LLM gets the full section for context. This is the right architecture for structured enterprise documents.

Document-specific chunking: for tabular data (financial reports, spreadsheets), chunk by row or by logical record rather than by text. For code, chunk by function rather than by line count.

The real-world lesson: a poorly chunked knowledge base produces retrievals where the right information is technically in the database but split across chunk boundaries so it's never retrieved as a coherent unit. Fixing chunking strategy often produces more improvement in RAG quality than any prompt engineering change.

* * *

**F5: How does access control work in an enterprise RAG system?**

This is a critical enterprise requirement that most RAG tutorials completely ignore. There are three layers to get right.

Document-level access control: only documents a user is authorised to see should ever appear in their retrieval results. The naive implementation — building one shared index for all users — is a security disaster. An employee must not retrieve confidential executive compensation data or another team's unreleased product plans.

The implementation options are: per-user or per-group indexes (simple but expensive to maintain and update), metadata filtering at query time (each document chunk has access control metadata — department, clearance level, document classification — and the retrieval query filters on these before returning results), or a post-retrieval access check (retrieve candidates, then check each against the user's permissions before passing to the LLM). Metadata filtering is the most scalable approach for large enterprises.

Row-level and field-level access: for databases and structured data, access control must go deeper than document level. A sales rep should retrieve their own account data, not all accounts. This requires the RAG query to be parameterised with the user's identity and filtered at the data source level, not just at the retrieval layer.

Audit logging: every retrieval query, every document accessed, and every response generated must be logged with user identity and timestamp. In regulated industries this is not optional — it is a compliance requirement. The RAG system must be designed with audit trails from day one, not retrofitted later.

The governance model: access control for the RAG system should mirror the access control of the underlying data sources. If a document lives in a SharePoint site that only HR can access, the corresponding chunks in the vector store should only be retrievable by HR users. Synchronising these permission systems is an ongoing operational challenge that must be planned for.

## 🟡 APPLIED QUESTIONS

* * *

**A1: How would you design a RAG system for a legal team doing M &A due diligence across 10,000 documents?**

This is a multi-phase retrieval problem, not a single query problem. The architecture needs to support several distinct query types simultaneously.

Data ingestion: documents arrive in a virtual data room (VDR) in heterogeneous formats — PDFs, Word documents, spreadsheets, emails. The first challenge is extraction quality. Scanned PDFs need OCR. Tables in financial documents need to be extracted as structured data, not as raw text. I would use a document intelligence service for extraction, with quality validation on the output before indexing.

Chunking strategy: legal documents require hierarchical chunking — the full agreement is the root, sections are parent chunks, individual clauses are leaf chunks. This allows queries like "retrieve the indemnification clause" to return the specific clause while giving the LLM the section context needed to interpret it correctly.

Index design: I would build multiple specialised indexes rather than one general index. A clause index (each chunk is a specific clause with metadata: clause type, agreement type, counterparty, governing law, effective date). A risk signal index (chunks representing identified risk language, pre-labelled by clause type). A numerical data index for financial terms, cap amounts, and dates — these are better retrieved by structured query than by semantic search.

Query types to support: targeted clause extraction ("find all limitation of liability clauses"), comparative analysis ("how do the change-of-control provisions differ across these five acquisition agreements"), anomaly detection ("which agreements contain unusual indemnification language compared to our standard form"), and timeline analysis ("which representations and warranties survive closing and for how long").

Access control: due diligence data is extremely sensitive. Each deal should have its own isolated index. Access is role-based — deal team members, external counsel, and executives may have different access tiers within the same deal. Every query is audit-logged.

Why not fine-tuning: the document set is unique to each deal and constantly growing as new documents arrive in the VDR. The model needs to reason about this specific deal's documents, not about legal concepts in general. Fine-tuning cannot achieve this; RAG over the deal-specific index can.

* * *

**A2: How would you use RAG to help a sales rep prepare for a competitive deal?**

The core insight here is that sales preparation is a multi-source retrieval problem that humans currently solve by manually searching through disconnected systems. RAG unifies these into a single query interface.

Data sources to index: competitor battle cards (updated monthly by product marketing), win/loss analysis reports (quarterly), customer case studies organised by industry and use case, past deal notes from CRM filtered by similar deal characteristics (deal size, industry, competing vendor), pricing exception history and the outcomes of those deals, and product capability documentation for the features most relevant to competitive deals.

Query design: a rep asks "I'm going into a deal against Competitor X in the financial services sector at $500K ARR — what do I need to know?" The system needs to retrieve across multiple indexes simultaneously: the battle card for Competitor X, the three most recent win/loss analyses involving Competitor X in financial services, case studies in financial services where we displaced Competitor X, and pricing exceptions granted in similar past deals.

Retrieval architecture: this is a query decomposition problem. The single natural language query should be decomposed into sub-queries targeting each index. A query router identifies which indexes are relevant; each is queried independently; results are merged and reranked by relevance before passing to the LLM for synthesis.

Why RAG over fine-tuning: competitive intelligence is updated constantly as products evolve. A fine-tuned model trained on last quarter's battle cards would be confidently wrong about a competitor that released a major product update last month. RAG retrieves the current battle card every time.

The freshness requirement: battle cards, win/loss analyses, and pricing data must be re-indexed promptly when updated. I would implement a document change detection system (webhook from the content management system) that triggers re-embedding and index update within minutes of a source document change.

* * *

**A3: How would you design a RAG system for finance teams monitoring loan covenants?**

This is one of the cleanest RAG use cases because it combines structured data retrieval with unstructured document retrieval, and the consequences of getting it wrong are severe and concrete.

The problem structure: a company has multiple debt facilities, each with a covenant package — financial maintenance covenants (leverage ratio must be below 4.5x, interest coverage ratio above 2.0x, etc.), reporting covenants (must deliver audited financials within 90 days of year end), and negative covenants (cannot make acquisitions above $50M without lender consent). Currently, a treasury analyst must manually look up each covenant definition in the credit agreement and compare it to the latest financial data.

RAG architecture: two primary data sources. First, the structured covenant index: each covenant is extracted from the credit agreement and stored as a structured record with fields for covenant type, metric definition, threshold, testing frequency, and the exact clause text. Second, the financial data index: current period financial metrics from the ERP or financial data warehouse, updated daily. This second source is better implemented as a tool call to a live database query rather than a static vector store, since financial data changes constantly.

Query pattern: "Are we in compliance with all covenants on the Facility B credit agreement as of this month?" The system decomposes this into retrieving the covenant list for Facility B, retrieving the current metric values for each covenant, then asking the LLM to reason about compliance status for each one and flag any that are close to breach (say, within 15% of the threshold).

Why RAG is architecturally correct: the covenant definitions are locked in a legal document that is thousands of words long. The LLM cannot memorise this company's specific covenant package through fine-tuning. Standard prompting would require pasting the entire credit agreement into every query context. RAG retrieves only the relevant covenant clauses for the specific question being asked.

The alert design: the most valuable output is not just "are we compliant today?" but "at what trajectory will we breach, and which covenant is closest?" This requires the RAG system to retrieve both current metrics and historical trend data, enabling the LLM to project forward.

* * *

**A4: How would you use RAG for a compliance team mapping a new regulation to internal controls?**

When a new regulation is published — say, a new data localisation requirement or an update to GDPR guidance — a compliance team must determine which internal controls are affected, whether existing controls are sufficient, or whether new controls must be created. Currently this takes weeks of manual analysis.

Data sources: the regulatory text itself (chunked by article and recital), the internal control inventory (each control described with its purpose, the requirement it addresses, the owner, and evidence of operation), past regulatory mapping work from previous regulatory changes, and external regulatory analysis from law firms and industry bodies.

RAG query pattern: "A new regulation requires companies to delete customer data upon request within 48 hours. What existing controls do we have that address this, and where are the gaps?" The system retrieves the relevant control descriptions that relate to data deletion and subject access rights, the relevant clauses from the new regulation, and any prior regulatory analysis on similar requirements. The LLM then reasons about coverage and gaps.

The gap analysis is the non-obvious high-value output: rather than asking "what do we have?", the query is structured as "compare what this regulation requires against what our controls currently cover and identify the difference." This requires the retrieval to bring in both the regulatory requirement text and the control documentation simultaneously.

Freshness architecture: regulatory text is published on government websites and in the official gazette. An automated feed monitors these sources, triggers ingestion when new documents are published, and alerts the compliance team that a new regulatory item has been indexed and is ready for analysis.

Why not standard prompting: the LLM has no knowledge of this company's specific internal control inventory. Even if it knew the regulation (from training data), it cannot reason about the gap between the regulation and this specific company's controls without retrieving that control documentation.

## 🔴 ADVANCED QUESTIONS

* * *

**Adv1: What are the main failure modes of an enterprise RAG system and how do you mitigate each?**

This is the question that separates senior candidates from junior ones. There are six distinct failure modes, each requiring a different mitigation.

Retrieval failure — the right document exists but is not retrieved. Causes: poor chunking (the answer is split across chunk boundaries), poor embedding model choice (the model doesn't represent the domain vocabulary well), or insufficient recall (top-k too low). Mitigations: hybrid retrieval combining dense and sparse search, hierarchical chunking, domain-specific embedding models fine-tuned on the document corpus, and increasing top-k with a reranker to filter.

Faithfulness failure — the LLM generates claims not grounded in retrieved documents. The hallucination problem. Mitigations: explicit prompting to answer only from retrieved context, faithfulness scoring in the evaluation pipeline, structured output forcing the LLM to cite specific chunks for each claim, and a secondary LLM judge that flags uncited claims before the response reaches the user.

Staleness failure — the retrieved document is outdated. The knowledge base contains last year's policy when this year's version has been published. Mitigations: document versioning with active version tagging, automated ingestion pipelines triggered by source document changes, and expiry metadata on time-sensitive chunks that flags them for review.

Access control failure — a user retrieves documents they should not see. Mitigations: metadata filtering at retrieval time enforcing permission boundaries, never trusting the LLM to apply access control (it must be enforced at the retrieval layer), and regular access control audits comparing vector store permissions against source system permissions.

Context poisoning failure — a maliciously crafted document in the knowledge base manipulates the LLM's output. A prompt injection attack via the retrieval path. Mitigations: input validation on ingested documents, sandboxed LLM execution that cannot take actions based on retrieved instructions, and monitoring for unusual response patterns.

Relevance degradation over time — as the knowledge base grows, retrieval quality degrades because the signal-to-noise ratio falls. Mitigations: regular knowledge base curation (retiring stale or superseded documents), monitoring retrieval quality metrics over time (context precision tracked per query type), and periodic re-evaluation of the embedding model as better domain-specific models become available.

* * *

**Adv2: How do you handle data freshness and index staleness in a production enterprise RAG system?**

Data freshness is one of the most underestimated operational challenges in enterprise RAG. Most architectures handle ingestion at setup but have no systematic answer to "what happens when documents change?"

The freshness taxonomy: different document types have different update frequencies and different consequences of staleness. A policy document updated annually with low query volume can be refreshed weekly. A competitor battle card updated monthly used by 500 sales reps daily needs near-real-time refresh. A regulatory text that changes rarely but where staleness could cause a compliance violation needs change monitoring even if updates are infrequent.

Update detection patterns: for documents in managed systems (SharePoint, Confluence, Google Drive), use webhook subscriptions or change notification APIs to trigger re-ingestion immediately when a document is modified. For external sources (regulatory websites, competitor press releases, news feeds), use scheduled crawlers with change detection based on content hashing. For database-backed content (financial metrics, CRM data), use database change data capture (CDC) streams to update derived knowledge base records.

The re-ingestion pipeline: when a document changes, the old chunks must be deleted from the vector store and new chunks ingested. This requires a document-to-chunk mapping maintained as metadata — you cannot just re-index everything when one document changes in a corpus of millions.

Versioning strategy: for legal and compliance documents, it is valuable to retain previous versions in a versioned index so users can query "what did the policy say before the January update?" Active queries route to the current version by default; historical queries can target specific version snapshots.

Staleness signalling to users: where freshness is critical, surface the document date in the response so the user can assess whether the retrieved information is current. A compliance officer asking about a regulatory requirement should see "this answer is based on guidance last updated 14 months ago" rather than receiving an answer with no temporal context.

* * *

**Adv3: How do you enforce row-level access control in a RAG system that serves multiple user roles?**

This is the hardest access control problem in enterprise RAG and the one most commonly implemented incorrectly.

The naive mistake: building one shared index and relying on the LLM's system prompt to say "only return information relevant to the user's role." This is not access control. An adversarial prompt can bypass it. The LLM cannot reliably enforce data boundaries. Access control must be structural, not instructional.

The correct architecture: each document chunk stored in the vector store must carry access control metadata — at minimum: owner department, document classification level, and list of authorised roles or groups. At query time, the retrieval request includes the user's identity and roles. The vector store's metadata filter restricts the candidate set to only chunks the user is authorised to see before the embedding similarity search runs. The LLM never sees documents the user is not authorised to access.

Implementation in practice: most production vector databases (Weaviate, Pinecone, Qdrant, pgvector) support metadata filtering. The filter is applied server-side before semantic search, not as a post-retrieval step. Post-retrieval filtering is insufficient because the LLM may still reference information from unauthorised chunks in its intermediate reasoning even if those chunks are removed from the final context.

Synchronisation with source systems: the metadata filters in the vector store must stay synchronised with the access control lists in the source systems. If a user's role changes, their accessible chunks in the vector store must update accordingly. This requires an identity synchronisation pipeline — typically integrated with the corporate identity provider (Azure AD, Okta) via SCIM or event streaming.

The audit requirement: every query must be logged with user identity, the access control filters applied, the document chunks retrieved (by ID), and the response generated. This audit trail is required for GDPR compliance (right to know what data was accessed), SOC 2 audit evidence, and internal security reviews.

**Adv4: What is hybrid retrieval in RAG and when should you use it over pure dense vector search?**

Hybrid retrieval combines dense retrieval (embedding-based semantic search) with sparse retrieval (keyword-based BM25 or TF-IDF search) and uses a fusion algorithm to merge the results.

Why pure dense retrieval fails in enterprise contexts: enterprise documents contain technical terminology, product names, regulatory article references, clause numbers, and proper nouns that embedding models handle poorly. "Article 17 GDPR" and "right to erasure" are semantically related in the domain, but an embedding model trained on general text may not represent them as close vectors. BM25 will reliably find "Article 17" by exact match; dense search will find related concepts by semantic similarity.

The fusion mechanism: the two retrieval systems return separate ranked lists. Reciprocal Rank Fusion (RRF) is the standard merging algorithm — it combines the rank positions from both lists without requiring score normalisation. The merged list is then passed to a cross-encoder reranker that scores each candidate against the full query for final ordering.

When to use each approach: dense-only is appropriate when queries are always natural language, the domain vocabulary is close to general language, and exact term matching is not critical. This is rare in enterprise contexts. Sparse-only (traditional search) is appropriate when users always know exactly what they're looking for and use precise terminology. Also insufficient for most enterprise use cases. Hybrid is appropriate for the vast majority of enterprise scenarios — specifically any domain with specialised terminology, regulatory references, contract clause numbers, product names, or code identifiers.

The reranker: after fusion, a cross-encoder reranker (e.g. a small transformer fine-tuned for relevance ranking) scores each retrieved chunk against the full query. This is more accurate than bi-encoder similarity scores but computationally expensive, so it's applied only to the top 20-50 candidates from fusion, not to the full index.

**Adv5: How do you evaluate and continuously monitor a production enterprise RAG system across multiple use cases?**

Offline evaluation (pre-deployment): build a golden dataset for each use case with curated question-answer pairs and expected source documents. Compute RAGAS metrics — context recall, context precision, faithfulness, and answer relevance — for each domain. Set minimum thresholds as quality gates: a change to the chunking strategy or embedding model must not decrease faithfulness below 0.80 or context recall below 0.75 on any golden dataset before deployment.

Online evaluation (production monitoring): log every production query with the retrieved chunks, the response, and user feedback signals (thumbs up/down, follow-up clarification queries, escalation to human). Use implicit feedback — a user immediately rephrasing their question is a signal of retrieval failure. Compute rolling metrics on a sample of production traffic using LLM-as-judge evaluation.

Per-function monitoring: each business function has different quality requirements and different failure costs. Compliance and legal functions have very high failure costs — a wrong answer about a regulatory requirement has legal consequences. These functions warrant dedicated golden datasets, tighter thresholds, and human-in-the-loop review for low-confidence responses. Sales and HR functions can tolerate higher variance.

Retrieval quality monitoring specifically: track context precision (what fraction of retrieved chunks were actually used in the response?) over time. Declining context precision indicates the knowledge base is accumulating noise. Track the percentage of queries where the system says "I don't have that information" — a sudden spike indicates a coverage gap in the knowledge base, often because a source document was removed or a new topic area has emerged.

Alerting: set automated alerts for: faithfulness score dropping below threshold on a rolling 7-day window, context recall declining week-over-week, user escalation rate increasing, and queries returning zero results (complete retrieval failure). These should route to the RAG platform team for investigation before they become visible user-facing degradations.

**Adv6: How do you handle multi-hop reasoning requirements in an enterprise RAG system?**

Multi-hop reasoning is when answering a question requires retrieving from multiple documents and chaining the reasoning across them. This is extremely common in enterprise contexts and extremely poorly handled by naive RAG implementations.

Example: "Which of our suppliers are located in countries where we have active litigation?" Answering this requires retrieving the supplier list with country of origin (from the procurement system), retrieving the list of countries where active litigation is ongoing (from the legal matter management system), and then reasoning about the intersection. No single retrieved document contains the answer.

Why naive RAG fails: a single vector search for "suppliers in countries with active litigation" will return documents that are semantically close to this query but will not actually contain the answer, because the answer doesn't exist in any single document — it has to be derived.

Solution — iterative retrieval (agentic RAG): rather than a single retrieval step, implement a reasoning loop. The LLM first retrieves the supplier list, then formulates a new retrieval query based on what it found ("retrieve litigation records for countries: Germany, China, Brazil"), retrieves the second document set, and then reasons over the combined evidence. This requires the RAG system to be implemented as an agent with tool-calling capability rather than a single retrieval pass.

Solution — query decomposition: before retrieval, a query decomposer breaks the multi-hop question into atomic sub-questions, each of which is independently retrievable. The sub-questions are executed in sequence (when later questions depend on earlier answers) or in parallel (when independent). Results are aggregated before the final LLM reasoning step.

The latency trade-off: iterative retrieval adds multiple round-trips, each with LLM inference and vector search latency. For an enterprise use case where accuracy is paramount and latency tolerance is measured in seconds rather than milliseconds (a compliance analyst asking a complex question), this is acceptable. For a customer-facing chatbot where sub-second response is required, multi-hop retrieval is impractical and the use case should be redesigned.

## Quick Reference: RAG Architecture Decision Framework

Decision point| Key question| Answer guides  
---|---|---  
RAG vs fine-tuning| Does the knowledge change frequently? Is it private?| If yes to either → RAG  
Chunking strategy| Is the document structured (contract, policy) or unstructured (article, email)?| Structured → hierarchical chunking. Unstructured → semantic chunking  
Retrieval type| Do users use precise technical terms or natural language?| Both → hybrid retrieval. Natural language only → dense  
Index design| Is this one use case or multiple?| Multiple → separate specialised indexes per domain  
Access control| Are different users authorised to see different data?| Yes → metadata filtering at retrieval time, never at prompt level  
Freshness strategy| How fast does the source data change?| Hours → CDC/webhook ingestion. Days → scheduled refresh. Weeks → manual trigger  
Evaluation| Is this a high-stakes use case (legal, compliance)?| Yes → human-in-the-loop review, tighter thresholds, dedicated golden dataset  
Multi-hop| Does answering require combining multiple documents?| Yes → agentic RAG with iterative retrieval or query decomposition
