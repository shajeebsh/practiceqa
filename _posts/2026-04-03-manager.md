**DATA ANALYTICS & TECHNICAL**

**Q4:   ****Walk me through how you would design a Product Analytics model from scratch.**  
---  
  
**Model Answer**

I start with the business question, not the data. I'd sit with Product, Sales, and Customer Success to understand what decisions they need to make and on what cadence. From there, I identify the key metrics — activation rates, retention, feature adoption, revenue attribution — and define them precisely in a data dictionary.

Next, I map the data sources: product event data, CRM, billing, support tickets. I design the data model using a Gold-layer star schema — fact tables for events and transactions, dimension tables for users, products, and time. I implement CDC pipelines to ensure freshness, and build the BI layer in Tableau or Power BI with role-based access so each team sees what's relevant to them.

Finally, I validate with stakeholders before go-live. A model no one trusts is a model no one uses.

**TECHNICAL DEEP-DIVE QUESTIONS**

**Q4** _  —  Technical_ **Walk me through the most complex data pipeline you've built. What made it hard and how did you solve it?**  
---  
  
**▸****Model Answer**

The most complex pipeline I've built was at Travelers Insurance — an end-to-end CDC pipeline using Snowflake Streams and Tasks to process Medicare and Medicaid claims data in near real time.

What made it hard: the source data had schema inconsistencies across payer systems, claims arrived out of order, and the downstream fraud detection team had a hard SLA of under 15 minutes latency. Any pipeline failure had direct business consequences.

My solution was three-layered. In the Bronze layer, I ingested raw claims with full schema flexibility using a semi-structured approach, preserving source fidelity. In the Silver layer, I applied standardisation and deduplication logic using Streams to capture only changed records — avoiding full table scans on 100M+ row tables. In the Gold layer, I built aggregated fraud-signal models that the detection team could query sub-second using properly sized Snowflake virtual warehouses.

The hardest technical challenge was handling late-arriving records without corrupting aggregated metrics. I solved this using a watermark-based late-data correction mechanism that reprocessed the affected time windows without a full pipeline restart.

Result: latency dropped from 24 hours to under 15 minutes, and manual review effort fell 40%.

**Q5** _  —  Technical_ **How do you ensure data quality across a complex multi-source data environment?**  
---  
  
**▸****Model Answer**

My philosophy is: catch bad data as early as possible and make quality visible to the business, not just the engineering team.

Practically, I implement quality checks at every layer. In the ingestion layer: schema validation, null checks, referential integrity. In the transformation layer: row-count reconciliation between source and target, statistical distribution checks to catch anomalies in numeric fields, and cross-source consistency checks where the same entity appears in multiple systems.

At AXA, I built automated PySpark-based monitoring in Microsoft Fabric that scored each dataset on a quality index and surfaced failures directly into the Power BI executive dashboard — so the actuarial team could see data freshness and quality alongside their KPIs, not just trust they were getting clean data.

I also document data quality rules in the STTM alongside transformation logic. This means when a new analyst joins or a new data source is added, the quality standards are explicit and enforceable, not tribal knowledge.

The most important thing I've learned: data quality is a product feature, not a hygiene task. When stakeholders can see it, they invest in it.

**Q6** _  —  Technical + Stakeholder_ **A business stakeholder tells you the numbers in your dashboard are wrong. How do you handle it?**  
---  
  
**▸****Model Answer**

First, I take it seriously and respond quickly — even if I'm 99% confident the data is right. A stakeholder who doesn't trust the dashboard won't use it, regardless of technical accuracy.

My process: I ask them to show me the specific number they believe is incorrect and what they expect to see instead. Often this immediately reveals whether it's a data issue or a definition mismatch — for example, they're comparing 'gross revenue' to our 'net revenue' metric.

If it's a genuine data issue, I trace the lineage from the dashboard metric back through the Gold, Silver, and Bronze layers until I find the discrepancy. I always do this with documentation — so the fix is auditable and I can explain exactly where it went wrong.

If it's a definition issue, I treat it as a governance opportunity: update the STTM, add a tooltip to the dashboard, and schedule a 15-minute alignment session with the wider team so everyone shares the same definition going forward.

I then close the loop with the stakeholder personally — not just fix it silently. That follow-through is what rebuilds trust.

**Q7** _  —  Product Analytics_ **How do you approach designing metrics and KPIs for a product analytics function?**  
---  
  
**▸****Model Answer**

I start with the business decision, not the available data. The question I ask stakeholders is: 'What would you do differently if this metric moved 20% in either direction?' If the answer is 'nothing', it's not a KPI — it's a vanity metric.

I structure metrics in three tiers. Strategic metrics — the north star indicators that tell leadership whether the product is winning: revenue, merchant retention, acceptance rate. Operational metrics — the leading indicators that predict strategic performance: feature adoption, API uptime, data latency. Diagnostic metrics — the drill-down views used by analysts when a strategic metric moves unexpectedly.

Every metric gets a formal definition in a data dictionary: business definition, SQL computation, owner, refresh cadence, and known caveats. This prevents the situation where Sales and Finance are each calculating 'revenue' differently and arriving at conflicting numbers in the same meeting.

At J&J I applied this exact framework — it took three weeks of stakeholder workshops upfront but saved months of confusion downstream.

**Q8** _  —  Technical_ **Describe your experience with real-time or near-real-time data processing.**  
---  
  
**▸****Model Answer**

I've delivered near-real-time pipelines in two distinct environments. At AXA, I implemented stream monitoring on claims data using Microsoft Fabric, reducing detection latency from 24 hours to under 15 minutes. The challenge here was handling variable claim volumes — burst periods during peak filing times — without pipeline degradation. I solved this using auto-scaling compute in Fabric and pre-aggregated Bronze-layer summaries to reduce downstream processing load.

Choosing the right pattern for the requirement is as important as the technical implementation.

**Q12** _  —  Leadership_ **Describe how you run a successful analytics project from brief to delivery.**  
---  
  
**▸****Model Answer**

I run analytics projects in five phases. Discovery: I spend the first week doing structured stakeholder interviews — what decision does this enable, who uses it, what does 'good' look like, what's the cadence. I resist the urge to go straight to the data.

Design: I draft a one-page analytical spec — metrics definition, data sources, proposed model structure, delivery format — and get sign-off before writing a line of SQL. This prevents rework.

Build: I work iteratively in two-week sprints, sharing early drafts with one trusted stakeholder rather than waiting for a 'big reveal'. Feedback early is cheap; feedback at delivery is expensive.
